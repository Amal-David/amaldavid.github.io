What I'm trying to build

I'm on the quest to build a reliable, fine-tuned script writer — a small LLM (something on the order of a Qwen 3.5 mid-size with a LoRA on top) whose output hands any downstream TTS a script which already knows what it's supposed to feel like. Not "produce text and hope the TTS guesses the emotion." Rather: a model whose output has the affective contour, the cue tags, the drawn-out vowels, and the per-segment direction baked in, so when an Eleven, Gemini, or open-source TTS reads it, what comes out is something a human voice actor would recognise as expressive.

Concretely, the script writer needs to learn to emit at least four layers of paralinguistic detail that conventional script generation throws away:

  • Drawn-out vowels and tonal holds as literal spellings in the text — heyyy, noooo, spaaaace, okaaaay, yesss, haaard, maaaybe. The number of repeated vowels carries information about hold duration and emphasis. Most ASR pipelines (CrisperWhisper included) normalize these to single-vowel forms; for the writer to emit them, the training corpus has to preserve them.
  • Non-verbal vocal cues as inline tokens — uh, ah, er, erm, um, uh-huh, mm-hm, huh, huhhh, hmm, oh, wow, well, plus the longer-form versions when held (uhhhh, mmmmm). These are the texture of real conversation; their placement is the difference between a flat read and a believable one.
  • Bracketed event tags covering the audible non-speech events — [laughs], [giggles], [chuckles], [sighs], [breath], [gasp], [throat clear], [cough], [whispers], [shouts], [singing] — placed at the exact word boundary where they occur, not as global "apply to whole sentence" markers.
  • Director's-note metadata at the paragraph level — tone, register, pace, primary emotion, per-speaker affect — so the writer can produce a comedy podcast monologue, a meditative guided breath, a stand-up bit, or a scene of two old friends gently roasting each other, all from the same model with different scene direction.

And the writer has to do this across the actual emotional range a real voice covers — not just happy/sad/angry/neutral but the blended states that show up in honest conversation: amused-resigned, hopeful-skeptical, excited-anxious, warm-sarcastic, tired-but-still-trying. The earlier "emotion gap" section spells out why current TTS systems can't reach those states from a flat transcript alone, and why a script writer that puts the affect where it goes in the script is the leverage point that doesn't require waiting for TTS itself to get better.

"Expressive" is the load-bearing word, and the genuinely hard part. The goal isn't "monologue for one tired podcaster." It's the full surface area of human speech a script could legitimately ask of a downstream voice:

  • Comedy. Setup-and-punchline timing. Laugh placement. Cross-talk between hosts. The deadpan-on-top-of-hyped layering you hear on Smartless.
  • Meditation. Slow pace. 2-3 second silences that mean something. Vowels held for a full second. The breath-spaced delivery a Tara Brach script needs.
  • Conversational podcast. Backchannels. Mid-sentence pivots and false starts. The em-dash hesitations that signal a speaker softening or catching themselves. Code-switching into a regional or cultural register.
  • Movie / scene dialogue. Distinct speaker voices. Affect that shifts across a beat. Whispered confidences, shouted exchanges, the silence between two characters that lands harder than either line.
  • Songlike delivery. Lyrical rhythm. Held notes inside speech. The texture of spoken-word performance.

And under all of those, the emotion spectrum a real voice carries: warmth, amusement, anger, frustration, anxiety, resignation, sarcasm, fatigue, grief, hopefulness, curiosity. And the blended states that are most of human affect (amused-resigned, hopeful-skeptical, excited-anxious). The earlier "emotion gap" section shows why the existing tag-based TTS systems can't do most of this on their own. The script-writer is the layer that says "here is exactly which emotion goes where in the script", and the downstream TTS — whatever it is — gets to focus on rendering instead of guessing.

And critically: this whole thing has to be doable in open source. Closed APIs are fine for prototypes; they're not how I want to build this. The corpus that trains the script writer should be derived from open-source models. The script writer itself should be an open-source LLM (LoRA on a Qwen 3.5 mid-size). The downstream TTS can be closed for now — Eleven, Gemini, OpenAI all produce good output. But the script writer should generate scripts portable across closed and open TTS alike. That's the only way to keep iterating on it without surrendering the data pipeline to a vendor.

That set of constraints — full-genre, full-emotion-spectrum, end-to-end open-source is what makes the rest of this post relevant. The TTS bake-off later shows what targets currently exist (and what the script writer has to be able to drive). The audio-LLM bake-off below shows where the open-source annotation tier is right now. The schema decides what the script writer is going to learn from. Each piece is one node of the same question.

So the immediate, narrower question of this post: where do the training annotations come from? Hand-labeling 100 hours of audio at the granularity I need would take months. Frontier audio LLMs (Vertex Gemini 2.5 Pro, specifically) do this annotation beautifully but cost money and lock you into a closed API. Self-hosting an open-source audio LLM that gets close enough to Gemini would let me scale the corpus without rate limits, without sending audio to a third party, and with a path to fine-tune the annotator itself later. That's the build that's actually documented here.

Where the field is right now

It's worth being honest about state-of-the-art before claiming any gap. As of May 2026, audio LLMs and TTS are in two different places.

Audio understanding has moved fast. Frontier closed models — Vertex Gemini 2.5 Pro, OpenAI gpt-realtime — can listen to a 5-minute podcast and produce a structured description of what's happening: speakers, emotions, vocal events, even the specific drawn-out words. Open-source has been catching up at roughly one credible new model per quarter — Qwen3-Omni in Q1, Kimi-Audio in late 2025, MiDashengLM in early 2026. The open audio-understanding gap to Gemini is shrinking.

Audio synthesis, on the production-controllable side, has also moved. The discrete-enum era is over for frontier closed TTS: ElevenLabs v3 exposes ~70 inline tags including [tired], [hesitates], [deadpan]; Hume Octave 2 takes free-text prosodic direction; Google Gemini 3.1 Flash TTS accepts scene-level direction inline. You can mark different parts of a sentence with different affects. But the ceiling moved, it didn't disappear. Every closed system still applies one uniform style across each tagged region. Continuous within-span affect, sarcasm, the smirk-while-talking. These are still unsolved. And open-source TTS sits roughly 18 months behind closed frontier on emotion expressiveness.

So the picture: open-source audio understanding is close to closed-source for structured listening. Open-source synthesis is far behind for expressive rendering. The corpus + script-writer project lives in that asymmetry — I can build the training data from open-source listening, and bet that synthesis catches up (or that even closed synthesis renders my scripts better than a baseline writer's scripts).

The moment that started this benchmark

With the goal set and the plan picked, the obvious next move was to try the most-praised open audio LLM and start labeling clips. The first model I reached for was Audio Flamingo Next, NVIDIA's 8B audio model. On the first run, it returned identical output for every clip. Same speaker count. Same tone description. Same four breath events at exactly the same timestamps. NPR news, a Moth story, a meditation, a stand-up set, all returned the same JSON. The model wasn't broken, exactly. It just wasn't listening. The audio kwarg was being silently dropped by the processor.

That was the moment I realized model leaderboards are not the same as model deployments. The number on the HuggingFace page is one promise. The number you get on your data, in your container, with your wiring, is a very different one. This post documents the gap, and the seven candidates I ran end-to-end to close it.

A note up front: I'm not an academic, I don't work at a frontier lab, and I don't have a research background in speech. Most of what follows was reasoned from first principles — read the model card, read the wrapper's source, try the obvious thing, watch it fail, read the error, try the slightly-less-obvious thing. If a real audio researcher reads this and winces, that's fair. I'm posting it because there isn't enough writing online about what actually breaks when you take an open audio model from "load it in a notebook" to "produce structured outputs on real podcast audio in Modal." This is one outsider's build log, not a methodology paper, and I'd genuinely love to hear what I got wrong.

So when I obsess later in the post about whether the annotator scores 0.71 or 0.79 against Gemini, what I'm really obsessing about is the quality of the training data for the downstream model. A noisy annotator means a noisy corpus means a script writer that learns to put [laughs] in the wrong places. Calibrating this one box is the single highest-leverage decision in the whole pipeline.

What you just heard, technically (the chunk-by-chunk drift problem)

If you tried to reproduce that em-dash pivot with ElevenLabs v3 by chunking the line — first half [confident], second half [warm, softer]. It would sound terrible. Every tag boundary becomes a tiny acoustic seam, like a video where the camera filter swaps every three seconds. There is, as it happens, a NeurIPS 2025 paper that names this exactly: WeSCon (arXiv:2509.24629) calls these "unnatural acoustic discontinuities at segment boundaries" and even proposes a quantitative measure for them (DNSV (variance of DNSMOS-Pro across the utterance). Other 2025/2026 work attacks the same target from different angles: TED-TTS aims for "smooth intra-utterance emotion shifts", TTS-CtrlNet conditions on time-varying emotion via ControlNet, Microsoft's EmoCtrl-TTS / "Laugh Now Cry Later" uses continuous valence/arousal trajectories instead of discrete labels, and CoCoEmo names the deeper limitation: single-label utterance-level control "collapses affective diversity".

The gap: even the best closed TTS lets you tag regions, but each region still applies one uniform style. Continuous within-span affect, what humans do constantly — is unsolved.

Real human affect is rarely one label. The textures in this 25 seconds:

  • Hype + dry self-mockery: "we're so excited" is performed for the audience while the follow-up "I gave Sean a big smack" undercuts it. The same speaker holds both registers in one breath.
  • Co-host pile-on as comedic affect, three voices echoing "one more crack at it" is itself an emotional unit; no single tag captures group-banter rhythm.
  • Performative-warm: "thank you for coming out tonight" is sincere AND ironized by the surrounding chaos.

Sentence patterns that exercise the same problem in writing:

  1. "I really appreciate you scheduling this meeting at 5:30 on a Friday — truly, what a thoughtful use of everyone's time." The hinge is "truly". Tag the whole thing [grateful] and it's wrong; tag the second half [sarcastic] and the transition is a cliff.
  2. "So the migration is done, the rollback plan is tested, and we're ready to ship — I mean, I think we're ready, mostly, unless QA found something this morning." Confidence drains across the sentence. No clean tag boundary.
  3. "Okay, okay, the offer letter is here, it's actually here, oh god, I have to decide by Monday." Excitement and anxiety are co-present from word one.

Three approaches to a complex line: (A) one global tag. The joke dies. (B) chunked tags — seams audible, tokens wasted. (C) a paragraph of scene direction at the top, then the sentence flows — affect as a continuous trajectory.

I've started calling the third approach the "director's note" framing — direction at the scene/paragraph level (like screenplay stage directions) instead of per-word tag chunks. There's no academic name for this yet, but Hume's Octave 2 documentation gestures at it (their natural-language style field is literally called "Acting Instructions"), and Bott et al. (Interspeech 2024) compare natural-language prompts to discrete tags directly and find prompt-style direction produces more natural prosody. Voice acting works this way too: a director gives one paragraph of intent before a scene, not [smiling] over each word.

A drawn-out vowel as duration vs spelling. The audio carries the signal; the standard transcript erases it. The corpus has to recover it from the audio directly.

Why this matters for the script writer: if I want a fine-tuned LLM to emit "spaaaace" or "noooo" or "heyyy" in the right places, the training data has to contain those spellings. Standard ASR throws them away. So the audio-LLM annotator (the subject of the rest of this post) becomes responsible for re-injecting them, by listening for duration ratios over a threshold and emitting the elongated orthographic form.

The two-speaker correction. And how the pipeline derived it

An honest mistake I made on first pass: I treated The Read clip as a single-speaker rendering, defaulting to one female voice.   2. Gender labeling per speaker: I cross-checked with the audio (and with the speakers' real-world identities — Crissle West and Kid Fury). speaker_0 = F, speaker_1 = M. No STT system in this benchmark returns gender natively; deriving it from voice would require a separate classifier (audeering/wav2vec2-age-gender, for example), which I haven't wired in yet. The point isn't that "we got the speakers right." The point is that this is the only way to do it right. The speaker IDs come from one model (Scribe), the gender labels come from another (or a human, for now), and the voice routing is a manual mapping from speaker_id × gender → lab-specific voice. End-to-end, this is the gap between "STT returns text" and "TTS recreates the audio faithfully". And it's not closed by any single product in May 2026.

Where accent goes (and why no STT gives it to you)

One thing the audio comparison made very visible: no production ASR returns accent or dialect information. I verified this across Deepgram Nova-3, ElevenLabs Scribe v2, OpenAI gpt-4o-transcribe, and CrisperWhisper. Every one returns either a BCP-47 language tag or just text. None of them returns "Black American", "Southern AAVE", "British RP", or anything similar. Open-source accent classifiers exist (the SpeechBrain CommonAccent model covers 16 English accents) but AAVE isn't a class in any of them and the major APIs haven't shipped this yet.

The practical consequence shows up immediately in the TTS row. The Read is hosted by Crissle West and Kid Fury (Black Americans who code-switch into AAVE constantly. If I feed a clean transcript to a default TTS voice, I get a generic white-American reading of words that were never written for that accent. The "spaaaace" and "[laughs]" tags I add in the enhanced row don't fix the accent. They just sit on top of the wrong base voice. So for The Read I had to do accent manually:

  • ElevenLabs: searched the shared voice library for African American female voices, picked CiCi ("true southern black woman's voice with the sweetness of your African American homegirl"). Voice selection does the work; no inline accent tag needed.
  • Gemini 3.1 Flash + 2.5 Pro: the 30 named voices are all accent-neutral General American. So accent has to live in the natural-language preamble: "Speak in a Black American voice with Southern AAVE inflection. This is the speaker's native register." Gemini's own docs recommend the most-specific framing possible ("African American English as heard in the American South" > "Black accent"). Adherence is hit-or-miss; 3.1 Flash is noticeably better at this than 2.5 Pro.
  • OpenAI gpt-4o-mini-tts-2025-12-15: the instructions field accepts free-text accent direction. I switched the voice from marin (a neutral warm female) to coral (more flexible per OpenAI's own examples) and added "Speak in a Black American voice with Southern AAVE inflection." to both clean and enhanced. Adherence is the weakest of the four. The underlying voice timbre stays largely fixed; only prosody shifts.

Honest framing for the post: accent-aware TTS isn't a production capability in May 2026. It's a marketing claim layered on inference-time prompt steering. The end-to-end pipeline "audio → STT → derived accent → TTS picks matched voice" doesn't exist as a shipped product. Every lab routes accent through manual mapping, and adherence on under-represented accents (AAVE specifically) is brittle. For corpus work, the realistic answer is voice cloning the actual speaker, which Eleven exposes via Professional Voice Cloning but Gemini and OpenAI don't. That's another gap to close before the script-writer can reliably emit "speaker = Crissle West" and have the downstream TTS render her voice.

Why no production TTS handles these well

Subtler affects beyond these three are even further behind. Two 2025 papers — "Modeling Sarcastic Speech" and the bi-modal sarcasm-feedback work: argue sarcasm is qualitatively harder than canonical emotions because it requires modeling the divergence between semantic content and prosodic delivery. No production TTS exposes a controllable knob for sarcasm, for irony, for smirk-while-talking, or for the polite-chuckle-versus-genuine-laugh distinction. Closed models can sometimes improvise these from prompt context; nothing reproduces them on demand. And open-source TTS — Bark, Sesame CSM, Kyutai Moshi, XTTS-v2 descendants, sits ~18 months behind closed frontier on emotion expressiveness.

The thread running through all three layers above: the affective contour the human voice produces continuously is, at every current TTS tier, approximated by discrete styling, even when that styling is hidden behind tags or natural-language prompts. My side of the bet is narrower than solving TTS: build a corpus and a fine-tuned open-source script writer whose training data carries the affective contour finer than the global tag, and pair it with paragraph-level scene/style metadata so the writer learns when to describe affect (director's-note style) versus when to mark it inline. The schema's tone_description field captures scene-level direction; per-speaker emotion + timestamped vocal events capture local shifts. The hope is that with both, the writer produces output that a downstream TTS — closed or open — can render with something closer to a continuous affect contour. Whether the TTS itself renders the contour is somebody else's problem; my job is not to throw the signal away at the script-writing step.

The problem

I'm building a paralinguistic-rich training corpus to fine-tune an open-source LLM into a TTS-aware script writer. A model that, given a prompt, can emit a script with Bark-style cue tags ([laughs], [breath], [pause:0.5s]) and drawn-out vowels like "heyyy" and "noooo" in the right places, so downstream TTS engines like ElevenLabs v3 or Gemini's TTS render expressive audio rather than monotone reads. To build that corpus, I need an audio annotator: a model that listens to 5 minutes of audio and writes a structured JSON description of what it heard. Those annotations become the supervised-learning labels for the script writer.

Vertex Gemini 2.5 Pro does the annotation beautifully. About $0.05 per 5-minute clip, ~35 seconds wall time, output is rich and accurate. But Gemini is a paid API. At pilot scale (100 hours = 1,200 clips) Gemini costs ~$60 — fine. At 10,000 hours it's $6,000 and a rate-limit dance. More importantly, you can't fine-tune Gemini for our specific taxonomy. I needed an open-source candidate good enough to run on Modal.

The question I had to answer before scaling: is there an open-source model that gets close enough to Gemini that I can self-host without dropping below a quality threshold I'd actually use? The answer turns out to be yes, three of the seven open candidates passed the bar. But getting there required solving four classes of bugs that none of the model cards mention.

Each field, and why it earns its place in the schema:

n_speakers and register. The structural skeleton

Before any tone or emotion decision, the script writer has to know whether it's generating a single-host monologue or a multi-party dialogue. These are categorically different generation problems. A monologue has long uninterrupted spans; a 3-host podcast has turn-taking, backchannels ("yeah", "mm-hm"), interruptions, overlaps. The register field (monologue / dialogue / multiparty) picks the generation mode; n_speakers tells the model how many distinct voices to script. Without this signal, the fine-tune learns an "averaged" structural style that fits nothing well.

primary_emotion_overall + tone_description. The affect anchor

Two fields for the same axis because they cover different timescales. primary_emotion_overall is the categorical anchor (10 emotion labels: amused, calm, excited, curious, sarcastic, etc.) that the script writer can condition on at the prompt level. tone_description is a free-text one-liner ("warm and measured; takes long pauses for emphasis") that captures nuance the categorical field can't. And that nuance is exactly what the per-segment emotion-shift work the previous section described needs as supervision. A 10-way label is too coarse; a 70-tag vocabulary like Eleven v3's is too prescriptive. The free-text tone description sits in between, and the script writer can learn to map it to specific delivery patterns.

primary_emotion_per_speaker, multi-host affect divergence

A separate per-speaker emotion field, because in any 3-host podcast clip the affective texture comes from contrast: one host is excited, the other is dryly skeptical, the third is curious-but-confused. If you collapse to one global emotion, you lose the comedy. The script writer needs to be able to script a sentence where Host A is [playfully] and Host B is [deadpan] in response, and this field is the supervision signal for that.

vocal_events — paralinguistic punctuation

The literal [laughs], [breath], [sigh], [gasp] markers, each with a timestamp and the speaker who produced it. These map 1:1 to inline cue tags in Bark-format scripts. Without this field, the fine-tuned writer will produce sterile-sounding scripts even when the source genre is laughter-rich. Where the laugh occurs (mid-sentence vs sentence-end) matters as much as whether a laugh occurs — early in the sentence reads as defensive, end-of-sentence reads as warm. Timestamp granularity is what turns a categorical "is there laughter?" into "should this script have a laugh after the punchline word?"

drawn_out_words. The expressive vocabulary ASR throws away

This is the field I care about most, and the one most ASR pipelines silently destroy.

A drawn-out vowel survives in the audio as duration but vanishes in conventional transcripts. Recovering it is one of the explicit goals of the corpus.

"Heyyy", "noooo", "ohhhhh". These aren't typos. They're how humans signal warmth, mock-protest, dawning realization. Most transcription pipelines (including CrisperWhisper, which is otherwise excellent at preserving disfluencies) normalize them to "hey", "no", "oh" at the ASR layer because the dictionary doesn't have entries for the elongated spelling. The information is still in the audio (a duration ratio of 2.5x or 3x against the CMU-dict expected vowel length), but it's gone from the transcript. This field re-injects it: when an annotator hears "heyyy" lasting 850ms instead of the expected 280ms, it emits the elongated spelling so the script writer's training data has the long-form version to learn from. (The downstream forced-aligner does the actual timing work via NeMo-FA on phoneme boundaries; this schema just records the orthographic surface.)

estimated_pace — articulation rhythm

The script writer uses pace to pick pause lengths and sentence rhythm. A very_slow Tara Brach meditation gets long pauses, short clauses, soft consonants. A fast stand-up set gets clipped sentences, no pauses, dense punchline density. The categorical pace label is a coarse but useful conditioning input; the underlying numeric (syllables per second from MFA alignment + CMU-dict syllable counts) is in a separate enrichment vector for the actual fine-tune.

content_topic + notable_paralinguistic_moments. The why and the when

Two metadata fields that don't directly drive script generation but are essential for training-data filtering. content_topic is a one-line semantic summary (e.g. "a meditation teacher inviting the listener to settle into a sense of heart-space"). It lets me cluster the corpus by topic at training time so the fine-tune sees balanced exposure across genres rather than 80% comedy podcasts. notable_paralinguistic_moments is a free-text list of specific moments the annotator found striking, used to weight high-information records higher during fine-tuning.

What about the things I didn't include?

An honest list of fields I considered and rejected for v1, because every "yes" has an opportunity cost:

  • Continuous valence / arousal / dominance per word. The right way to do emotion granularity. But it requires a frame-level emotion model (emotion2vec+ or audeering's wav2vec2-msp-dim) at inference time, not the LLM annotator. It lives in a separate enrichment vector, not this schema.
  • Per-word pitch accents: H*/L+H*/!H* labels via Wav2ToBI. Same reason: lives in the audio-feature side of the pipeline, not the LLM annotation.
  • Per-clip music_present and low_quality_audio flags: included originally; cut from the schema shown above because all 7 golden clips are clean speech. They'll come back when the corpus expands to scraped podcasts.
  • Speaker demographics (gender, age, accent): important for corpus balance, but the audio-LLM annotators are inconsistent at these and Gemini specifically declines to guess. They get derived from a dedicated wav2vec2-age-gender classifier at the audio-pipeline stage.

The principle: this schema captures what the LLM annotator is reliably good at — coarse structural and affective labels at the clip level. And explicitly defers fine-grained acoustic features to specialized models downstream. A bad calibration here would be making the schema too rich, then watching three of the seven annotators fail to fill half the fields, and ending up with a benchmark that just measures "which model gives up first."

To score one model's annotation against another, I compare each field. Gemini is the soft ground truth. Six dimensions feed into the confidence score: n_speakers, register, primary_emotion, laugh_presence, breath_presence, drawn_out_present. Each disagreement reduces confidence. Acceptance threshold: 0.70.

Using Gemini as ground truth is a known compromise — Gemini has its own biases and isn't always right. But on a 7-clip benchmark, hand-labeling everything to a true ground-truth would take longer than building the entire bake-off. Gemini agrees with my ear on the dimensions that matter, and the bake-off is comparative anyway: we care whether another model matches the reference, not whether the reference itself is perfect.

The ASR-tier baseline, what you get before the audio-LLM tax

The rows marked [ASR] aren't audio LLMs; they're speech-to-text systems. I added four of them — ElevenLabs Scribe v1, Deepgram Nova-3, OpenAI gpt-4o-transcribe, and CrisperWhisper, not because they're competitors to Qwen3-Omni and friends but because they answer a different, prior question: before you pay the audio-LLM tax, what does cheap-and-fast already give you?

The results are more interesting than I expected. ElevenLabs Scribe v2 at 0.746 sits above the 0.70 acceptance gate: landing between Kimi-Audio (0.75) and MiDashengLM (0.71). Scribe v2 (the January 2026 model, replacing v1's 0.705) pulls this off by being the only ASR in the lineup that detects audio events as inline transcript tokens: [laughs], (audience cheering), (applause). On Smartless it captured 28–32 audio events; on stand-up, 38–46. None of the other ASRs detect any. Deepgram Nova-3 lands at 0.699 — fractionally below the gate, by nailing diarization (no audio LLM does diarization better) but offering nothing on laughter/breath/elongation. OpenAI gpt-4o-transcribe and CrisperWhisper both score 0.643 on the Vector J rubric, but for different reasons: OpenAI returns only clean transcript with no diarization (and on Smartless it actively truncated the audio to one sentence after getting confused by overlapping voices), while CrisperWhisper, once wired up with the official nyrahealth recipe (custom transformers fork, explicit AutoModelForSpeechSeq2Seq load, and the adjust_pauses_for_hf_pipeline_output utility. My first attempt skipped the utility, and the model emitted zero filler tokens) actually produces 61 filler tokens across the 7 clips (32 in Smartless, 11 in Reply All, 10 in The Read, 6 in NPR, 1 each in Moth and stand-up, 0 in Tara Brach). The Vector J rubric doesn't reward filler-token detection directly, so the aggregate score doesn't reflect what it gained. But for our downstream pipeline, the filler tokens matter.

One thing none of the four ASRs do: emit elongated spellings. No "heyyy", no "spaaaace". CrisperWhisper detected zero across all 7 clips even with the proper recipe; Deepgram's docs explicitly say "Deepgram will never transcribe 'uhhhh' instead of 'uh'"; OpenAI and Eleven normalize too. This is the gap the corpus pipeline has to close manually. And the way I plan to do it is duration-ratio analysis on forced-aligned phonemes: run NeMo-FA (or MFA at phoneme level) on each utterance, look up the CMU-dict expected duration of each vowel, and emit elongated spellings whenever the observed duration exceeds the expected by some threshold (typically 1.8x or 2x). It's not the only way to handle this — you could also do it from a frame-level prosody model or by fine-tuning an ASR to emit the long form natively. But duration-ratio on forced-aligned phonemes is the one I've found is cheapest to do well, and it's what feeds the script-writer's training data downstream. None of this lives in the ASR tier; it has to be a separate pass.

What the ASR tier is structurally incapable of, by feature surface:

  • Deepgram has "sentiment" (positive/negative/neutral polarity) and "intents" and "topics" but no affect categories. On The Read it classified the host as negative, intent "Express frustration". The host is being warm and reminiscent about childhood. Polarity sentiment misclassifies affective tone. Its filler-words docs explicitly say "Deepgram will never transcribe 'uhhhh' instead of 'uh'" — drawn-out vowels are normalized at the tokenizer.
  • OpenAI gpt-4o-transcribe returns only text. No diarization, no events, no sentiment. Cleanest output of the four, fewest dimensions answered.
  • ElevenLabs Scribe returns text + diarization + audio events. No emotion field; no per-speaker affect.
  • CrisperWhisper returns text with [UH]/[UM] filler tokens and word timestamps. Best disfluency F1 on the market. No diarization, no events, no emotion.

Honest framing: the ASR tier is not a competitor to the audio LLMs but a complementary floor. A production architecture probably runs Scribe or Deepgram to get diarization + transcript + (maybe) event tags, then routes only the labeled spans through an audio LLM for paralinguistic enrichment. That hybrid is ~30× cheaper than running Qwen3-Omni on the full corpus, and it's what the leaderboard's combined ranking points toward.

Costs span 240×, from SenseVoice at $0.002/clip to Qwen3-Omni at $0.48/clip. That's the difference between $2.40 and $576 at 100 hours of audio, or $24 vs $5,760 at 1,000 hours. The choice of annotator becomes the dominant cost driver of the entire corpus pipeline at any non-trivial scale.

Three points anchor the frontier:

  • SenseVoice ($0.002, 0.672): below the acceptance gate on its own but useful as part of a hybrid. It detects laughs and breaths well; it doesn't diarize. Pair it with a cheap LLM to fill in the rest of the schema and you might rebuild a 0.75+ candidate at $0.003/clip.
  • MiDashengLM ($0.04, 0.709). The value sweet spot. Just clears the gate, costs 12× less than Qwen3-Omni. Surprised us. The model was designed for audio captioning, not structured annotation. Once we found the right load pattern (more on that below), it worked.
  • Qwen3-Omni ($0.48, 0.792). The premium pick. ~10% more confidence than MiDasheng, 12× the cost. Worth it if you're scoring against rare distributions where the extra 8% matters.

What broke and how we fixed it

Half the work was code; the other half was making the models load at all. Each model had its own debugging chain. I've collapsed the deep technical details into accordions below. Skip past these if you don't care about specific Modal image recipes; the lesson takeaways live just below this section. Expand the ones you need.

1. Audio Flamingo Next. The silent input dropout (1 hour · wrong audio wiring pattern)

The opening anecdote. I'd been passing audio as a separate audios= kwarg, which is the pattern most HuggingFace audio models accept:

inputs = processor(text=PROMPT, audios=wav, sampling_rate=16000, return_tensors="pt")

Audio Flamingo's chat template expects audio embedded inside the conversation list:

conversation = [[{
    "role": "user",
    "content": [
        {"type": "text", "text": PROMPT},
        {"type": "audio", "path": str(audio_path)}
    ]
}]]
batch = processor.apply_chat_template(
    conversation, tokenize=True,
    add_generation_prompt=True, return_dict=True,
).to(model.device)
batch["input_features"] = batch["input_features"].to(model.dtype)

With the wrong wiring, the audio was silently dropped. The model produced the same hallucinated response for every clip because the input was effectively identical. The diagnostic: send the model two distinctly different clips and ask for transcripts. If you get the same transcript twice, the wiring is wrong.

Lesson 1. Always sanity-check that an audio model is actually listening by running it on two clips you know are different. Compare the outputs. If they match, the audio isn't reaching the model.
2. Qwen3-Omni. A four-bug debugging chain (afternoon · flash-attn, processor, dtype, return shape)

Qwen3-Omni was four bugs stacked, each surfaced only after fixing the previous:

  1. flash-attention required at the audio encoder. Setting attn_implementation="sdpa" on the LLM body doesn't help; the audio tower hardcodes flash-attn. Fix: install prebuilt cp311 wheel for torch 2.6 / cu12 from the Dao-AILab releases page.
  2. StopIteration in replace_multimodal_special_tokens. The processor expected an audio-lengths iterator but received empty. Root cause: the process_mm_info helper from qwen_omni_utils returned no audios because the messages format was subtly wrong. Fix: skip process_mm_info, load audio with librosa, pass as audios=[wav] directly to the processor.
  3. float vs bfloat16 mismatch. Processor returns float32 tensors; model is bfloat16. Fix: explicitly cast input_features and feature_attention_mask to model.dtype after the processor call. (This same fix matters for MiDashengLM — see below.)
  4. "not enough values to unpack (expected 2, got 1)". Newer Qwen3-Omni's generate() returns a single tensor; older returns (text_ids, audio_ids). Fix: handle both at the unpack site with gen_out[0] if isinstance(gen_out, tuple) else gen_out.

Each was 5–15 minutes to diagnose plus a Modal redeploy. Cumulatively, an afternoon. None of these are in Qwen3-Omni's README.

3. MiDashengLM. The meta-tensor placement (2 hours · device_map + dtype alignment)

The error: RuntimeError: Tensor on device cpu is not on the expected device meta!. Model loaded fine; crashed on first inference call. I tried loading without device_map, with low_cpu_mem_usage=False, with explicit .to("cuda") after load. None worked. The standard PyTorch ritual of "load on CPU then move to GPU" is the wrong pattern for this model.

The fix, per a more careful reading of the README: device_map="auto" at load time, AND explicit .to(device=model.device, dtype=model.dtype) on the processor output. The custom audio encoder uses lazy meta-init for some buffers; the processor returns CPU-float32 tensors; the model expects CUDA-bfloat16 tensors. Aligning both sides at the chat-template boundary is the actual fix.

inputs = processor.apply_chat_template(
    messages, tokenize=True,
    add_generation_prompt=True, return_dict=True,
).to(device=model.device, dtype=model.dtype)

One more catch: MiDashengLM's generate() returns only the new tokens, not prompt + new as most models do. If you slice output_ids[:, prompt_len:] you get an empty tensor. The right thing is to decode the full output. I lost 45 minutes here too.

4. Kimi-Audio. The WhisperEncoder path bug (the deepest rabbit hole · monkey-patching the wrapper)

Kimi-Audio is distributed as a Python wrapper (kimia_infer) that handles tokenization and decoding internally. The wrapper expects to load the bundled Whisper-large-v3 weights from inside the Kimi-Audio repo. Concretely, line 172 of kimia_infer/models/tokenizer/whisper_Lv3/whisper.py:

self.speech_encoder = WhisperModel.from_pretrained(model_path).encoder

where model_path is the Kimi-Audio repo root: moonshotai/Kimi-Audio-7B-Instruct. But Kimi's repo has Whisper at a subpath, not the root. WhisperModel.from_pretrained doesn't know to look in the subdir; it reads safetensors at the root, finds nothing, and crashes with AttributeError: 'NoneType' object has no attribute 'get' when metadata.get("format") hits a None (GitHub issue #40).

The workaround took an embarrassing number of attempts. The clean fix: pre-download the standalone openai/whisper-large-v3 into the Modal image, then monkey-patch kimia_infer.models.tokenizer.whisper_Lv3.whisper.WhisperEncoder.__init__ to point its internal WhisperModel.from_pretrained at the standalone repo:

import kimia_infer.models.tokenizer.whisper_Lv3.whisper as kw
_orig = kw.WhisperEncoder.__init__
def _patched(self, model_path, *args, **kwargs):
    return _orig(self, "openai/whisper-large-v3", *args, **kwargs)
kw.WhisperEncoder.__init__ = _patched

Then Kimi loads. Then it OOMs on A10G (22 GB) because the wrapper holds both Kimi's 7B and Whisper's 1.5B encoder simultaneously. A100-40GB resolves that. Total: roughly two hours, three Modal redeploys, one monkey-patch I wouldn't have discovered without reading the kimia_infer source.

Lesson 2. When a model is distributed as "use this wrapper class, not raw transformers," go read the wrapper's source before you try. The bugs are usually in how the wrapper resolves paths, not in the model.

The JSON problem in depth

How structured outputs broke and how to constrain generation properly (4 failure modes · outlines schema · expand for detail)

Even with all the models loading and generating, about a third of outputs initially failed to parse as JSON. The failure modes were not what I'd expected:

  • Mode A — Truncation at max_new_tokens. Model emits valid JSON but runs out of token budget mid-array. Most common for Audio Flamingo, which loves to emit per-second breath events and quickly hits the cap.
  • Mode B — Repetition loops. Model emits the same vocal event 150 times in a row before hitting the cap. Output is technically parseable but semantically useless.
  • Mode C — Python dict syntax. Model emits {'n_speakers': 1, 'low_quality_audio': False} with single quotes and Python booleans. Not valid JSON. This is most often Audio Flamingo, suggesting it was trained on Python code interleaved with JSON.
  • Mode D — Syntax pollution. Corrupted strings like "and.\"()". Rare but irrecoverable.

My first instinct was post-processing: write a JSON repair script that finds the last balanced closing brace, strips trailing commas, closes pending brackets. That works for Mode A but plateaus at ~70% parse rate because it doesn't help with Mode B (repetition) or Mode C (Python dicts).

The right fix is to constrain the generation itself. The standard 2025 approach is outlines (or lm-format-enforcer for HF models that resist outlines' token-sampling hooks), which applies a JSON-Schema-derived finite-state machine to token selection during generation. The model can only emit tokens that produce valid JSON matching your schema. No truncation, no Python-dict mode, no repetition past the schema's array bounds:

ANNOTATION_SCHEMA = {
    "type": "object",
    "properties": {
        "n_speakers": {"type": "integer", "minimum": 1, "maximum": 10},
        "register": {"enum": ["monologue", "dialogue", "multiparty"]},
        "primary_emotion_overall": {"enum": [...]},
        "vocal_events": {
            "type": "array", "maxItems": 12,  # kills repetition loops
            "items": {...},
        },
        ...
    },
    "required": ["n_speakers", "register", "primary_emotion_overall"],
}

The maxItems: 12 alone kills the repetition loops dead. For models that don't play nicely with outlines (custom processors that intercept token sampling), a defensive post-process pass works: _extract_json() falls back to ast.literal_eval for Python-dict-syntax outputs, and a separate _truncate_repetition_loops() caps any vocal_events array with > 15 same-type events in a row at 12 entries. Together these get the bottom-tier models from 14–57% parse rate to ~100%.

Lesson 3. Don't try to repair LLM output after the fact when you can constrain the generation upstream. Grammar-constrained decoding via outlines or lm-format-enforcer is now the standard answer for structured outputs from any HF model.

Per-dimension breakdown

For each model × each Vector J dimension, count of clips where the model agreed with Gemini (matches/total). Reveals each model's strengths and weaknesses by capability axis.

The aggregate confidence score hides which capabilities each model actually has. The dimension-level view reveals four clusters of strength:

Diarization (n_speakers, register). Qwen3-Omni and Kimi nail this. They correctly identify single vs multi-host structure on all 7 clips. SenseVoice always says n_speakers=1 because it doesn't diarize at all. MiDashengLM gets the speaker count right but sometimes mislabels register (dialogue vs multiparty). Audio Flamingo Next is correct about half the time, suggesting it has speaker awareness but not stable diarization.

Emotion (primary_emotion). Qwen3-Omni wins. SenseVoice has a built-in 8-emotion classifier but its categories are stale (HAPPY/SAD/ANGRY/NEUTRAL); for a clip Gemini calls "amused" or "curious", SenseVoice has to flatten to "neutral", costing it confidence even when it's directionally correct.

Vocal events (laugh_presence, breath_presence). SenseVoice's specialty. It detects laughter and breath events at the tokenizer level. But for the same reason, it produces a lot of false positives: it tagged every clip with music_present: true because the meditation clip's ambient hum triggered its BGM detector. Kimi and MiDasheng are more conservative; Qwen3-Omni is the most calibrated, matching Gemini on event presence ~85% of the time.

Drawn-out vowels (drawn_out_present). Every model struggles. Gemini caught 8 drawn-out words in The Read (Haaaappy, daaaay, soooo, reeeally, baaadass, fiiiine, heeeell, craaaazyhead). Kimi caught 2 (uuuh, soooo). MiDasheng emitted the literal string "...up to 20..." as its drawn-out list. It copied the prompt example. Qwen3-Omni and SenseVoice left the field empty. This is the dimension that drove most of the v5 corpus effort in the first place; the open candidates aren't quite there yet.

Lesson 4. When picking a model, look at the per-dimension matrix, not just the aggregate score. A 0.78 confidence model might be 1.0 on speaker count and 0.5 on drawn-out vowels. And which axes matter depends on what you're using the annotations for.

Meta-observations

Twelve days of running this benchmark surfaced a handful of patterns that generalize past any specific model. In rough order of how often they came up:

The processor API matters more than the model

Every audio LLM has a custom processor. The thing that converts your audio file into model-input tensors. The processor is where most of the cross-model API divergence lives: audios= vs audios in the conversation list; raw file paths vs librosa.load'd arrays; 16 kHz vs 24 kHz; input_features vs audio_input vs spectrograms. The model itself is often a well-documented HF Transformers config. The processor is the documentation gap.

If you're integrating a new audio model, budget more time for processor wiring than for inference. A sanity-check ritual: load the processor + model, send a 5-second test clip, print the keys of the resulting batch. If you don't see something that looks like audio features, your wiring is wrong.

"Pre-built wheels" is the answer to flash-attn

Five of the seven models needed flash-attention. Building flash-attn from source requires the CUDA toolkit at build time, which Modal's default debian_slim base doesn't include. The clean answer: download a prebuilt wheel from the Dao-AILab releases page that matches your torch version + CUDA version + Python version exactly. flash_attn-2.7.4.post1+cu12torch2.6cxx11abiFALSE-cp310-cp310-linux_x86_64.whl is verbose but explicit.

If you're using NVIDIA's CUDA-devel base image to compile from source, expect 5–10 minutes added to every image build and a much larger final image. Wheels are faster.

The kimia_infer/transformers compatibility window is narrow

Kimi-Audio specifically refuses to load with arbitrary transformers versions. Older than 4.43 → no rope_theta attribute on the config. Newer than ~4.51 → the GenerationMixin internals shift. I landed on transformers==4.45.1 + huggingface_hub==0.23.4 because that's what zsxkib's working Replicate deployment uses. When a model's wrapper is older than the libraries it depends on, the deployment recipe usually exists somewhere — find the most recent working third-party deployment and copy its pin set verbatim. This is one of the few places where being an outsider helps: the people building wrappers professionally already solved the version-pin puzzle, and their Cog files are sitting on GitHub for anyone to read.

"Soft ground truth" is a viable strategy at this scale

Using Gemini as the ground-truth reference for the benchmark felt like cheating at first. But on a 7-clip benchmark, hand-labeling every dimension across every clip would take longer than the entire bake-off. Gemini's annotations match my ear on the dimensions that matter, and the comparison is relative anyway: what matters is whether another model matches the reference, not whether the reference is itself perfect. A real lab with budget would hire trained linguists to label every clip and reduce inter-annotator disagreement; I'm one person reasoning from first principles, so the soft-ground-truth shortcut is what makes the experiment possible at all.

The honest framing: this benchmark measures how well each open model imitates Gemini, not how well each open model annotates audio. They're different metrics. For a corpus that will train a TTS-aware script writer, "imitates Gemini" is good enough because Gemini's annotations are the corpus I'd use anyway if cost weren't a problem.

Cost dispersion is the real story

The 240× cost spread between SenseVoice ($0.002) and Qwen3-Omni ($0.48) is wider than the confidence spread (0.672 vs 0.792. A factor of 1.18×). On a long-tail axis, picking a cheaper-but-marginally-worse model is usually right, because the cost compounds linearly with corpus size while the quality difference plateaus. At 100 hours: $2.40 vs $576. At 1,000 hours: $24 vs $5,760. At 10,000 hours: $240 vs $57,600.

For our corpus, the right answer is probably MiDashengLM ($0.04, 0.71) for routine annotation with Qwen3-Omni ($0.48, 0.79) reserved for high-stakes clips where we want the extra precision. That cost-tier hybrid drops average cost to ~$0.06/clip while keeping quality near 0.78 on the clips that matter.

Audio LLMs are 12-18 months behind frontier APIs and closing fast

Qwen3-Omni at 0.79 is impressive. A year ago no open audio LLM would have hit 0.50 on this benchmark. Kimi-Audio launched in late 2025, MiDashengLM in early 2026, Qwen3-Omni in Q1 2026. The cadence is roughly one credible open audio LLM per quarter. By late 2026 or early 2027 I'd expect at least one Apache model to match Gemini-class audio understanding outright. For now, the gap is real but it's bridgeable for our specific task.

What this benchmark does not measure

I want to be honest about the limits, because a 7-clip benchmark is not production validation.

  • Seven clips is tiny. A real validation needs 50–100 clips across more accents, recording qualities, and registers. The pattern of disagreements would shift.
  • Gemini-as-ground-truth has bias. Gemini was trained on data that doesn't include some accents and registers well. When Gemini and a candidate disagree on a clip with heavy code-switching or strong non-American accents, it's not always clear that Gemini is right.
  • Vector J compares six dimensions, not every field. The drawn-out-words field is scored as binary presence rather than set overlap. A model that lists 8 and a model that lists 3 both pass the presence check; this hides the quality gap in the chart.
  • We did not measure: hallucination rate, factual accuracy of the content_topic field, latency under concurrent load, multi-language performance, or robustness to background music. All of these matter for production.
  • The cost numbers are list-price estimates. Sustained workloads on Modal can be cheaper with reserved capacity; cold-start dominates first runs.

Where this fits in the larger system

The annotator bake-off is one node in a bigger pipeline. The full corpus pipeline runs ~19 audio passes per clip (VAD, diarization, ASR, forced alignment, audio events, vocal sounds, emotion via emotion2vec+, pitch via CREPE, loudness via pyloudnorm, pacing, eGeMAPS acoustic features, phrasing, vowel elongation, and an assembler).

The LLM annotator from this bake-off is the soft ground truth on top of all that. When the local pipeline says one thing and the LLM annotator says another, the record is tagged low-confidence and downweighted during the eventual fine-tune. So picking the right annotator isn't cosmetic. It's the calibration anchor for the whole corpus.

That's why I cared enough to actually fix Kimi instead of declaring it blocked and moving on. The post lives or dies on whether I can say "I tried the obvious five candidates, here are real numbers across all of them, here is what I'd pick and why." Saying "I gave up on three" wasn't an acceptable form of the story.

What's next

With the annotator picked, the next step is scaling the pipeline to ~100 hours of audio across five open datasets (NonverbalTTS, EARS, Expresso, PodcastFillers, MELD) plus ~30 hours of newly scraped high-expressivity podcasts. Each clip gets the full audio pipeline plus the LLM annotator (likely Gemini for pilot, Qwen3-Omni for production scale). The output is one richly-annotated JSON record per clip.

Those records are the training data for the actual downstream task: fine-tuning an open-source LLM (probably a Qwen 3.5 mid-size model via LoRA, most likely PEFT on a single A100) into a TTS-aware script writer. The fine-tune takes a prompt and emits a script with inline cue tags — [laughs], [breath], [pause:0.5s]. And drawn-out vowels in the right places, so a downstream TTS engine can render expressive audio rather than monotone reads. The annotations described in this post are the supervised-learning labels for that fine-tune.

If the resulting script writer learns to emit "heyyy" and "noooo" and [laughs] the way the annotations describe them, this whole exercise, including the afternoon I spent monkey-patching kimia_infer — pays off. If it doesn't, at least the corpus exists in a form where another model can try.

The opening of this post was about a model that hallucinated identical output for every clip. The closing thought is that the cure for that, checking your work against ground truth, demanding that every component prove it's actually listening, treating "deployed" as different from "works" is the entire job of building with open models. The HuggingFace page tells you what's possible. The Modal logs tell you what's true.

And the deeper reason I cared enough to push through the Kimi monkey-patches and Qwen-Omni dtype hunts: the emotion-shift problem from the second section. Every casual claim that "TTS now has emotion" is technically true at the sentence level and not true at the sub-sentence level, and the people who hear the difference are the ones who use these systems for hours a day. Building a script writer that gets the affect contour right, even just at the open-source tier — feels like a small step that's mostly bottlenecked on supervised data, not modeling. So the data has to come from somewhere. Which is what this whole benchmark was for.

If you're picking an audio LLM for a production task, my tentative reading (very much an outsider's view, not a recommendation from someone with credentials): start with MiDashengLM at $0.04/clip, escalate to Qwen3-Omni at $0.48/clip for the hard cases. Add SenseVoice at $0.002/clip for paralinguistic event detection where Gemini-class is overkill. Skip Audio Flamingo entirely (research-only license). And budget more time than you think for the processor wiring, because that's where every model hides its true API.
